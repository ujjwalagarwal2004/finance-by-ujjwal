## First — What Even Is a T-Bill?

Imagine the Singapore government needs money to run the country. Instead of just printing cash, it borrows from everyday people like you. You lend it, say, S$10,000. Six months later, it pays you back S$10,000 plus a little extra — that "extra" is your return, and the whole arrangement is called a **Treasury Bill, or T-bill**.

It is one of the safest investments on earth because the borrower is the government — it is essentially impossible for them to not pay you back. No stock market swings, no company going bust. Just lend, wait, collect.

That's it. That's a T-bill.

Now, the return you earn on that loan goes up and down depending on global conditions — mainly what interest rates are doing around the world. And right now, those returns have quietly dropped to levels that should make every Singaporean rethink where their savings are sitting.

---

## What Happened to the High Returns?

Not long ago, a T-bill felt almost too good to be true. In 2022, yields hit a decade-high of **4.40%** — on money lent to the Singapore government, with virtually zero risk. Singaporeans lined up. Applications were oversubscribed. It was one of the best risk-free deals available anywhere in Asia.

**That era is over.**

Think of it this way: when global interest rates were high — mainly because the US was aggressively fighting inflation — Singapore's rates followed upward. Everything became unusually rewarding: T-bills, fixed deposits, savings accounts. Then the US Federal Reserve began cutting rates at the end of 2025, which put downward pressure on T-bill yields here too.

There's also a somewhat counterintuitive twist: amid concerns about a potential economic slowdown in Singapore, more investors flocked to the safety of T-bills — but more buyers chasing the same bonds means each buyer earns less.

The result: T-bill yields have fallen from that 4.40% peak all the way down to around **1.37% to 1.44% in 2026**. That is not a small dip. That is a 70% reduction in what you earn for the same level of risk.

## So Where Does Everything Stand Today?

Here is a plain snapshot of your main low-risk options right now:

| Option | What It Is | Return | Can You Exit Early? |
|---|---|---|---|
| CPF Ordinary Account | Your CPF money for housing | 2.5% p.a. | Tied to CPF rules |
| CPF Special / MediSave / Retirement | Your CPF money for retirement & health | **4.0% p.a.** | Partial, with conditions |
| Singapore Savings Bond (SSB) | A government bond you can exit anytime | ~2.14% p.a. (10yr avg) | Yes, no penalty |
| 6-Month T-bill | Short loan to the government | ~1.4% p.a. | No — fixed 6 months |
| Fixed Deposit | Money locked with a bank | ~1.5% p.a. | No — penalty if broken early |
| High-Yield Savings Account | Regular bank account with conditions | Up to 2.45% p.a. | Yes, fully liquid |

The number that stands out — and that most people walk past every day without realising — is that **4% on your CPF Special, MediSave, and Retirement accounts**.

## The CPF Angle Most People Ignore

Your CPF is not just a forced savings system you contribute to every month and forget about. The returns sitting inside it right now are, quietly, among the best guaranteed rates available to any Singaporean.

The Special, MediSave, and Retirement Accounts currently earn **4% per annum**, supported by a government-set floor rate extended until 31 December 2026. To put that in perspective — the government is guaranteeing you 4% at a time when a T-bill gives you 1.4% and a fixed deposit gives you 1.5%. There is no bank in Singapore matching that on a safe product.

It gets better:
- **Below 55:** You earn an extra 1% on the first S$60,000 of your combined CPF balances
- **55 and above:** The first S$30,000 earns an extra 2%, and the next S$30,000 earns an extra 1%

Some Singaporeans are effectively earning **5–6%** on a chunk of their CPF without doing a single thing.

The honest catch: CPF money is not freely accessible cash — it is structured for retirement, housing, and healthcare. But for money you genuinely do not need in the near term, this guaranteed return quietly compounds year after year, and right now it is beating almost everything else in the safe-money category by a wide margin.

---

## What About Singapore Savings Bonds?

The SSB sits in a sweet spot that does not get enough attention. Think of it as a government T-bill's more flexible cousin — also backed by the Singapore government, also essentially risk-free, but with one big difference: unlike fixed deposits where a penalty is charged for early withdrawal, **you can exit SSBs anytime without any penalty**.

The latest SSB gives you 1.40% if you hold for just one year, rising to a **2.14% average per year** if you hold for the full 10 years. The longer you stay, the more you earn — and if you change your mind, you simply cash out with no cost.

One practical bonus: demand for SSBs has been muted lately, with applications coming in well below the amount offered — which means most people who apply get the full amount they asked for, with no ballot or waitlist.

---

## What About Just Leaving Money in Your Bank Account?

Most standard bank savings accounts in Singapore pay somewhere between **0.05% and 0.1% per year** on your balance — the default rate if you do nothing. Some higher-yield accounts like OCBC 360 can offer up to 2.45%, but that typically requires you to credit your salary, spend on their credit card, and grow your balance each month. Even that rate is set to drop to 1.95% from May 2026.

The point is this: money sitting in a basic savings account is quietly losing purchasing power every year. Inflation in Singapore, even at modest levels, erodes what your cash can actually buy. **Doing nothing is not neutral — it is a slow, invisible loss.**

---

## So What Should You Actually Do?

There is no one-size answer, but here is a straightforward way to think about it depending on what the money is for:

**Money you might need in the next few weeks** — Keep it in a high-yield savings account. Liquidity matters more than rate here. Look at accounts that offer around 1.5–2% without too many conditions attached.

**Money you won't need for 1 to 10 years** — The SSB is hard to beat. Government-backed, no penalty to exit, and a 10-year average of 2.14%. In a low-rate environment, that combination of safety and flexibility is genuinely rare.

**Retirement savings you won't touch for years** — Maximise your CPF. Voluntary top-ups into your Special Account or MediSave let that 4% compound quietly over time. It may be the single highest guaranteed return available to you right now.

**Looking for meaningfully more** — That requires accepting actual risk: equity funds, REITs, or diversified portfolios. Those can return more over the long run, but they go down too. That is a separate conversation, and one that should only come after the above is sorted.

> The biggest mistake is not making a bad choice — it is making no choice and letting cash sit earning next to nothing while everything around it quietly grows.

## Glossary

**T-bill (Treasury Bill):** A short-term loan you give to the Singapore government, for 6 months or 1 year. You get your money back plus a small return. Considered one of the safest investments in existence.

**Singapore Savings Bond (SSB):** A government-backed bond you can hold for up to 10 years, earning more the longer you stay. You can cash out anytime with no penalty — which makes it more flexible than almost any other fixed-return product.

**Fixed Deposit:** You lock a sum with a bank for a fixed period at an agreed rate. Safe and predictable, but you usually face a penalty or lose interest if you withdraw early.

**CPF (Central Provident Fund):** Singapore's national savings system, where both you and your employer contribute a portion of your salary each month. Split into accounts for housing (OA), retirement and healthcare (SA, MA, RA).

**Interest Rate:** The percentage return you earn on savings, or pay on a loan. When it's high, your money grows faster sitting in safe places. When it's low, you need to work harder to find decent returns.

**Yield:** Another word for the return or interest rate on an investment. When a T-bill "yields 1.4%," that's what you earn annually on your money.

**Compounding:** When the interest you earn also starts earning interest. At 4%, S$10,000 quietly becomes around S$14,800 over 10 years — without adding a single dollar of your own.

**MAS (Monetary Authority of Singapore):** Singapore's central bank and financial regulator. It issues T-bills and SSBs, and oversees the entire financial system in Singapore.

**Inflation:** The general rise in prices over time. If your savings earn 0.1% but prices rise 2%, your money is effectively shrinking in terms of what it can buy — even if the number in your account looks the same.
