*How a government quietly spent $35 billion to stop its currency from collapsing — and why it still might not be enough.*

*Compiled from Reuters, OANDA, Barclays & OMFIF · May 1–2, 2026*

---

Japan's government spent the equivalent of **$35 billion** this week — quietly, without a press conference — to prop up the value of its own currency, the yen. The move triggered the dollar's sharpest weekly drop against the yen since February. If you have no idea what that means or why it matters, read on.

---

## The Basics — What Are We Actually Talking About?

Every country has its own currency — Japan uses the yen, the US uses the dollar. The rate at which you swap one for the other is the exchange rate. When the yen "weakens," it means one dollar buys you more yen than before — Japan's money has become cheaper relative to America's.

For most of the past year, the yen has been steadily losing value. This week it hit **160.7 yen per dollar** — its weakest since July 2024. That number alarmed Tokyo enough to act.

|---|---|
| 160.7¥ per dollar | The weak point that triggered intervention — worst since July 2024 |
| $35 billion | Estimated amount Japan spent buying yen back up, within roughly an hour |
| ~3% | Single-day yen surge on Thursday — biggest since January 2023 |
| 155.5¥ per dollar | Where the yen settled post-intervention, down from 158.3 |

## Root Cause — Why Has the Yen Been Falling in the First Place?

The answer comes down to interest rates. When a country's central bank sets high rates, investors worldwide park money there to earn more — which raises demand for that currency. The US Federal Reserve's rate sits around 4%. Japan's Bank of Japan has its rate at just **0.75%** — even after a historic hike in late 2025.

That gap drives a strategy called the **carry trade**: borrow cheaply in yen, invest in higher-yielding US dollar assets, pocket the difference. Investors doing this constantly sell yen and buy dollars — keeping the yen weak. Until that interest rate gap closes, the downward pressure doesn't go away, no matter how much Tokyo spends fighting it.

> *"A weak yen is perhaps inevitable given low interest rates and the so-called carry trade."*
> — OMFIF (Official Monetary and Financial Institutions Forum), 2025

---

## This Week's Events — What Did Japan Actually Do?

Governments have two tools when they dislike their currency's direction: **talk** (warn traders publicly) or **act** (buy their own currency using foreign savings). Japan used both in quick succession.

**Earlier in the week —** Japan's top currency diplomat Atsushi Mimura warned yen moves were "one-sided and sharp." Finance Minister Satsuki Katayama announced "decisive action" was coming — and told journalists to keep their phones close during the upcoming holiday. A remarkable public signal.

**Thursday, May 1 —** The yen hit 160.7 per dollar. Within an hour, it surged 3% to 155.5 — Japan had entered the market and bought yen in bulk. Bank of Japan data released the next morning suggested roughly **5.48 trillion yen ($35 billion)** was deployed. No official confirmation; markets pieced it together from the numbers.

**Friday, May 2 —** The dollar slid again. Mimura stated speculators were still active and Japan stood ready to return to the market. The dollar partially recovered to 156–157 yen by end of day.

**Weekly result —** The dollar logged its biggest weekly drop against the yen since February — roughly **1.25% for the week** — and the yen posted its largest weekly gain in the same period.

---

## The Holiday Risk — Why Japan's Golden Week Makes This More Dangerous

Japan is entering Golden Week — a stretch of national holidays where financial markets mostly close. With fewer traders active, currencies are easier to push around. Speculators have historically used this thin-trading window to pile pressure on the yen, knowing fewer institutional buyers are around to absorb the selling.

Japan intervened during Golden Week in 2024 too. The Finance Minister's smartphone remark was essentially a public warning shot: *we are watching, and we will strike again.* Barclays analysts noted the risk of further intervention remains "if USD/JPY rebounds sharply towards 160."

---

## The Honest Assessment — Will This Actually Stick?

In the short term, intervention works — it shocks traders, buys time, and deters the riskiest bets. But it cannot fix the underlying problem: **the interest rate gap is still enormous.** Markets are pricing roughly a two-thirds chance the BOJ raises rates again in June, which helps. But the Fed has shelved expectations of cuts, limiting how much the yen can recover on its own.

Most analysts agree the yen needs the BOJ's rate to reach at least **1.25–1.5%**, paired with Fed cuts, before any meaningful recovery takes hold. Neither looks imminent. As one strategist said plainly: *"The durability of intervention remains uncertain."*

---

## Real-World Impact — Why Does Any of This Matter?

**For Japanese households**, a weak yen means everyday imports — food, fuel, electronics — cost more. It squeezes budgets and is one key reason the government is so sensitive about the yen's level.

**For Japanese exporters** like Toyota and Sony, a weak yen is actually beneficial: their products become cheaper to buy abroad, and overseas earnings convert back into more yen. The Nikkei rose 1.5% this week partly because of this dynamic.

**For global markets**, the larger risk is a disorderly yen collapse triggering a mass unwinding of carry trades — trillions of dollars of bets that would ripple across stocks and bonds worldwide. That scenario is what keeps governments, traders, and analysts all watching the yen so carefully.

## Glossary

**Exchange Rate:** The price at which one currency swaps for another. At 155 yen per dollar, you hand over 155 yen and get 1 dollar back. When this number rises for USD/JPY, the yen is getting weaker.

**Currency Intervention:** When a government enters the foreign exchange market to buy or sell its own currency. Japan used its dollar savings to buy yen — raising demand and pushing the yen's value back up.

**Interest Rate:** The percentage you earn on savings or pay on loans. Central banks set a benchmark rate that ripples through the whole economy. Higher rates attract foreign money, which strengthens a currency.

**Carry Trade:** Borrow cheaply in a low-rate country (Japan at 0.75%), invest in a higher-rate one (US at ~4%), pocket the difference. Investors doing this constantly sell yen and buy dollars, keeping the yen weak.

**Bank of Japan (BOJ):** Japan's central bank, which sets Japan's interest rates and controls the yen supply — the Japanese equivalent of the US Federal Reserve.

**Federal Reserve (The Fed):** The US central bank. When the Fed keeps rates high, global investors prefer dollar assets, pulling money away from currencies like the yen.

**Verbal Intervention / Jawboning:** Officials making strong public statements to influence trader behaviour without actually spending money. The Finance Minister's "keep phones close" remark is a textbook example.

**Foreign Reserves:** A government's stockpile of foreign currency (usually dollars) accumulated over years. Japan used these reserves to buy yen during this week's intervention.

**Speculative Positions:** Bets placed by traders and hedge funds on which way a currency will move. When officials say "speculative positions are still evident," they mean traders are still betting the yen will keep falling.

**USD/JPY:** Shorthand for the US dollar vs. Japanese yen exchange rate. A rising number means the dollar is strengthening (yen weakening). A falling number means the yen is gaining ground.

**Golden Week:** Japan's cluster of national holidays in late April–early May. Thinner trading makes the yen more vulnerable to speculative attacks — which is why Japan has intervened during this period before.

**Real Interest Rate:** Interest rate minus inflation. Japan's real rate is deeply negative — meaning yen savings lose purchasing power — making the currency unattractive to hold versus US dollar assets.

**Policy Divergence:** When two countries' central banks move in opposite directions. Japan slowly raising rates while the US holds firm creates a persistent gap that pushes investors toward the higher-rate currency.

**Nikkei:** Japan's main stock market index (like India's Nifty 50 or the US S&P 500). A weaker yen often lifts the Nikkei because Japanese exporters earn more in yen terms when overseas revenues are converted back home.
