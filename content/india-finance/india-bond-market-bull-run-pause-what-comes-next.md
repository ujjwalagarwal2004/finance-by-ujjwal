## What Is a Bond Bull Market?

A bond is an IOU. When the government needs money, it borrows from investors by issuing bonds, promising to repay after a fixed number of years along with regular interest payments called **coupons**. The annual return an investor earns on that bond is called the **yield**.

The critical rule: **bond prices and yields move in opposite directions.** When lots of people want to buy bonds, prices rise and yields fall. A "bull market" in bonds means yields have been falling — which is good for anyone who already owns bonds, because their investment is now worth more.

When experts say "the bond bull market may pause but isn't over," they mean: yields have been trending downward for years, and while there may be a temporary hiccup, the broader direction is likely to continue.

---

## How India Got Here

India's 10-year Government Security (G-Sec) yield — the most important benchmark for borrowing costs in the country — has been on a long downward journey. It was stuck around 7.5–8% in 2015–16, broke below 7% when the RBI committed to rate cuts, dipped further through COVID-era stimulus, then rose again in 2022–23 as the RBI tightened policy to fight inflation. By early 2024, it was back around 7.2% — and the next leg of the bull market was about to begin.

| Period | 10-Year Yield | Driver |
|---|---|---|
| 2015–mid 2016 | ~7.5–8% | RBI holding rates; elevated inflation |
| Late 2016–2019 | ~6.5–7% | Rate cuts; demonetisation aftermath |
| 2020–2022 | ~6–7% | COVID stimulus, then RBI tightening |
| 2023–2024 | ~7–7.2% | RBI pause; inflation easing |
| Early 2025 | ~6.5% | RBI rate cuts; index inclusion flows |
| Late 2025 | ~6.6% | Sticky yields despite cuts; liquidity squeeze |
| April 2026 | ~6.98% | Yields drifting higher; market awaiting clarity |

## The 2024–2025 Acceleration

Three powerful forces drove bond yields sharply lower going into 2025.

**Inflation fell to a six-year low.** The RBI's job is to keep CPI inflation between 2–6%. India's headline CPI fell to 3.2% year-on-year in April 2025 — the lowest since August 2019 — with lower food prices being a key factor, as food makes up almost 50% of India's consumer price basket. With inflation comfortably inside its target, the RBI had a clear mandate to cut rates.

**The RBI pivoted to easing.** The RBI cut the repo rate by 25 basis points in February 2025, then made an additional 25 bps cut at the April meeting and shifted its stance from "neutral" to "accommodative." By June 2025, three cuts totalling 100 basis points had been delivered, reducing the repo rate from 6.5% to 5.5%. When the RBI cuts rates, investors rush to lock in existing higher yields before they disappear — that buying pressure pushes bond prices up and yields down.

**India joined the global bond club.** India's inclusion in the JPMorgan Global Bond Index in 2024 was a defining moment, further validated by the upcoming FTSE Russell index inclusion in September 2025. These developments boosted foreign portfolio investment and domestic confidence in the Indian debt market. Index inclusion is powerful because it is mechanical — passive funds that track these indices must buy Indian bonds automatically, creating a structural source of demand independent of short-term market sentiment. India is expected to account for approximately 9.35% of the FTSE Emerging Markets Government Bond Index, making it the second-largest country weight after China.

Adding to all this, the government lowered its fiscal deficit target to 4.4% of GDP for FY2025-26, signalling fiscal discipline that helped ease concerns about excessive bond supply — further supporting the case for stable or lower yields.

## Why the Rally Stalled in Late 2025

Despite all those tailwinds, the 10-year yield drifted back up to 6.98% by April 2026 — higher than where it started the year. This is the central paradox: the RBI cut rates by 125 basis points, yet long-term bond yields went up, not down. Several forces explain this.

**A banking liquidity squeeze.** A liquidity deficit of ₹2–2.5 trillion emerged in the banking system. Banks were prioritising lending over bonds as the credit-to-deposit ratio crossed 80%, and RBI foreign exchange interventions were further draining liquidity. When banks run short of cash, they stop buying bonds — and that removes one of the biggest buyers from the market.

**Heavy state-level borrowing.** While the central government managed its borrowing carefully, state fiscal pressures kept overall bond supply elevated through State Development Loans. Simultaneously, demand from long-term institutional investors like insurance companies and pension funds was waning due to regulatory changes — creating a supply-demand imbalance that kept yields elevated.

**Rupee pressure and global uncertainty.** Persistent rupee depreciation — driven by delays in the India-US trade deal, subdued capital inflows, and a widening trade deficit — significantly impacted market sentiment, putting the RBI in a difficult position balancing rate cuts, currency defence, and bond market stability.

**Geopolitical shock.** The India-Pakistan conflict in Kashmir in early May 2025 triggered foreign bond outflows, with foreign investors proving more prone to sentiment swings — while domestic investors continued to buy, leading to a divergence in market behaviour.

The core lesson here is that monetary policy works with long lags. Cutting the repo rate does not automatically bring down 10-year bond yields — especially when banks have a liquidity problem, states are flooding the market with new debt, and foreign investors are nervous.

## Analysis: Is the Bull Market Still Intact?

The long-term bull case remains intact. The near-term picture is messier.

**Why the structural case still holds:**
- Inflation is low and likely to stay contained, meaning the RBI has no reason to raise rates aggressively.
- Global index inclusion is a multi-year mechanical tailwind — passive foreign funds must keep buying Indian bonds as long as India remains in those indices.
- Fiscal consolidation means the government is borrowing less relative to GDP over time, easing long-term supply pressure.
- Even after rate cuts, Indian government bond yields remain among the most attractive in the Asia-Pacific region at around 6.6% for 7–10 year tenures.

**Why near-term risks are real:**
- Rate cut transmission has been less effective than expected, with yields remaining sticky at the long end of the curve despite the RBI's aggressive easing.
- Rupee vulnerability could trigger further foreign outflows.
- US tariff risks and geopolitical tension remain live threats to sentiment.

**Verdict:** The bond bull market is not over, but it has entered a range-bound, bumpier phase. Yields are unlikely to return to 8%+ without a severe inflation shock, but are equally unlikely to fall cleanly to 6% while the liquidity squeeze and supply imbalance persist. The most probable path for the 10-year G-Sec yield through the rest of 2026 is a 6.5–7.2% range, with a gradual downward bias as conditions normalise.

---

## The Bigger Picture

India's bond market stood at approximately ₹226.3 trillion (around USD 2.66 trillion) as of December 2024 — more than triple its 2014 size of ₹68 trillion. Yet India's bond market is still only around 70% of GDP, compared to 225% in the US and UK and 260% in Japan. That gap represents decades of structural growth ahead. The bull market, broadly speaking, is only in its middle chapters.

## Glossary

**Basis Point (bps):** One-hundredth of a percentage point. A 25 bps rate cut = a 0.25% reduction.

**Bond:** A loan to the government or a company, repaid with interest over a fixed period.

**Bull Market:** A sustained period of rising prices — in bonds, that means falling yields.

**Coupon:** The fixed annual interest payment on a bond.

**CPI:** Consumer Price Index — India's main inflation measure, with food making up ~50% of the basket.

**Fiscal Deficit:** The gap between government spending and revenue. A smaller deficit means less borrowing, less bond supply, and downward pressure on yields.

**G-Sec:** Government Security — a bond issued by India's central government. The 10-year G-Sec yield is the market's primary benchmark.

**Index Inclusion:** When a country's bonds are added to a global benchmark index, forcing passive funds worldwide to automatically buy those bonds.

**Liquidity:** The availability of cash in the banking system. Low liquidity means banks buy fewer bonds, pushing yields higher.

**Repo Rate:** The rate at which the RBI lends to commercial banks — its primary monetary policy lever.

**SDL (State Development Loan):** Bonds issued by state governments. Heavy state borrowing adds to overall bond supply and competes with central government G-Secs.

**Yield:** The annual return on a bond. Yield and price always move in opposite directions — when one goes up, the other goes down.
