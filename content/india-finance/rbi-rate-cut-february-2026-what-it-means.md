## What Just Happened

On 7 February 2026, the Reserve Bank of India (RBI) cut its **Repo Rate** from **6.50% to 6.25%** — a reduction of 25 basis points (0.25%).

This is the first rate cut since May 2020, when the RBI slashed rates to cushion the economy from COVID-19. For nearly five years after that, rates stayed flat or moved up. This cut signals a new phase.

## Why Did the RBI Cut Now?

Three main reasons came together:

**1. Inflation is under control**
India's retail inflation dropped to 4.4% in January 2026 — comfortably within the RBI's 2-6% target band. Food prices, which had been the main troublemaker, stabilised as vegetable prices corrected sharply from their 2024 highs.

**2. Growth needs support**
India's GDP growth slowed to 6.4% in FY2025 — still strong globally, but lower than the 8.2% of FY2024. The RBI wanted to give the economy a boost without risking price stability.

**3. Global rates are falling**
The US Federal Reserve had already cut rates three times in late 2024-early 2025. When global rates fall, India has more room to cut too without risking a major outflow of foreign money.

## What Changes for You

### Home Loans (Good News)

Banks will likely lower their home loan rates within 1-3 months. If your loan is on a **floating rate** (most are), your EMI should drop or the tenure should shorten.

On a ₹50 lakh home loan over 20 years at 8.75%, a 0.25% cut to 8.50% saves you about **₹830/month** and **₹1.9 lakhs total** over the loan life.

### Fixed Deposits (Mixed News)

Banks will also cut FD rates. If you're about to renew a 1-year FD, expect rates to drop from ~7% to around 6.75%. **Lock in FDs now before banks adjust rates** — especially if you rely on FD income.

### Equity Markets (Cautiously Positive)

Lower rates make borrowing cheaper for companies, boosting their profits. Lower FD returns also push investors toward equity. Both factors support stock prices — but markets had already priced in this cut partly, so don't expect a dramatic jump.

### Real Estate (Positive Over 6-12 Months)

Cheaper home loans mean more people can afford to buy. This is generally good for real estate demand, especially in the mid-segment (₹50L–₹1.5Cr range).

## Is This the Beginning of a Rate Cut Cycle?

Most economists and market analysts expect **1-2 more cuts in 2026**, bringing the repo rate to possibly 5.75-6%. This depends on:
- Monsoon quality (affects food prices)
- US Federal Reserve's next moves
- India's fiscal position (government spending and deficit)

The RBI has been careful not to over-commit. Governor Sanjay Malhotra described the stance as "neutral" — meaning future cuts are possible but not guaranteed.

## What Should You Do Right Now?

| If you have | Do this |
|---|---|
| Floating rate home loan | Wait — your EMI will likely drop automatically |
| FD maturing soon | Renew for 1-2 years before banks cut rates |
| Money in savings account | Consider moving some to a short-duration debt fund |
| SIP in equity funds | Do nothing — keep the SIP running, rate cuts are good for equities long-term |

## Jargon Glossary

**Repo Rate**: The interest rate at which the RBI lends overnight money to banks. When it falls, banks can borrow cheaper and pass the benefit to customers via lower loan rates.

**Basis Points (bps)**: A unit for measuring interest rate changes. 100 basis points = 1%. A 25 bps cut = 0.25% reduction.

**Floating Rate Loan**: A loan where the interest rate changes when the benchmark rate (like the repo rate) changes. Most Indian home loans are floating rate.

**Retail Inflation / CPI**: The rise in prices of everyday goods measured by the Consumer Price Index. The RBI targets 4% with a 2% tolerance on either side.

**GDP (Gross Domestic Product)**: The total value of all goods and services produced in a country in a year. India's GDP growing at 6.4% means the economy is 6.4% larger than last year.

**Monetary Policy Committee (MPC)**: A 6-member committee inside the RBI that votes on interest rate decisions every two months.

**Fiscal Deficit**: When the government spends more than it earns in tax revenue. A high deficit can be inflationary, which limits how much the RBI can cut rates.
