*The private sector bounced back in April. But it is still at its slowest sustained pace in four years. Here is what that means.*

*HSBC / S&P Global PMI Survey · May 5, 2026*

---

## First — What Is a PMI?

Every month, hundreds of Indian businesses are asked: are you getting more orders than last month? Hiring more? Producing more? The answers get compressed into a single number called the PMI.

The rule is simple: **above 50 = growing, below 50 = shrinking.** The higher above 50, the faster things are moving. India's has stayed above 50 for nearly five years straight — a remarkable run.

---

## What Happened in April

India's composite PMI rose to **58.3** in April from 57.0 in March, remaining well above the 50-mark that separates expansion from contraction. So on the surface — good news. India grew faster than the month before.

But here is the catch. The reading still marked the **second-slowest improvement in operating conditions in nearly four years.**

Think of a runner who has been gradually slowing down for several months. In April, they ran slightly faster than March — but they are still far off their usual pace. That is India right now.

---

## What Is Going Well

**Exports surged.** New export orders expanded sharply, with the pace of growth reaching a **seven-month high.** Companies reported improved demand from clients in Australia, France, Japan, China, Saudi Arabia, the UAE, and the UK.

**Hiring picked up.** Job creation reached a **10-month high**, indicating increased hiring by firms to meet rising demand — a sign businesses still feel confident enough to expand their workforce.

**Manufacturing led the charge.** The factory PMI bounced from 53.9 to **55.9**. Services held steady too.

---

## What Is Still Hurting

**The Middle East war.** India imports almost all of its oil. When conflict disrupts energy markets, fuel prices rise — and that cost runs through everything: raw materials, transport, production, and eventually your shopping bill. Input costs increased at the **fastest pace since August 2022**, and output prices rose at the quickest rate in six months.

**Cautious buyers.** Competitive pressures, the ongoing West Asia conflict, and hesitation among clients to approve quotations weighed on overall growth. Companies that would normally say yes to big orders are pausing and waiting.

**Softer domestic demand.** While exports are strong, spending inside India is more restrained. New orders from domestic customers increased at the **slowest pace in over three years**.

---

## The Bottom Line

India is not in trouble — a PMI of 58.3 is still healthy by any global standard, and the five-year streak of continuous growth is genuinely impressive. But the engine is not firing at full power.

The IMF recently set India's growth forecast at **6.5%**, but warned that inflationary risks could dampen the high-octane expansion seen in recent years. The April PMI fits that picture precisely: still growing, but with the brakes slightly on.

Whether India accelerates again depends on three things:
- Whether the **Middle East conflict** eases and energy costs come down
- Whether this year's **monsoon** is strong enough to boost rural spending
- How the **RBI** balances cutting interest rates against keeping inflation in check

## Glossary

**PMI (Purchasing Managers' Index):** A monthly business survey compressed into a single number. Above 50 = growth; below 50 = contraction. Watched closely because it is one of the earliest economic signals available each month.

**Composite PMI:** A combined score covering both factories and service businesses — giving a full picture of the entire private sector, not just one part of it.

**Input Costs:** What businesses pay to make their product — raw materials, fuel, electricity. When these rise, businesses either accept lower profits or raise prices for customers.

**Domestic Demand:** Spending happening inside India, as opposed to export orders coming from foreign buyers. When domestic demand slows, it signals that Indian consumers and businesses are being more cautious with their money.

**RBI (Reserve Bank of India):** India's central bank. Controls interest rates, which affect how easily businesses and consumers can borrow money — and therefore how fast the economy grows.
