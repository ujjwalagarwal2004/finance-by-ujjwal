*Tuesday, 29 April 2026 | FX & Macro Analysis*

---

### 01 — Executive Summary

The Indian rupee slipped to 94.47 per U.S. dollar on Tuesday, continuing its descent as elevated oil prices — driven by the effective closure of the Strait of Hormuz amid the U.S.–Iran standoff — weighed on energy-importing Asian currencies broadly. State-run banks stepped in to sell dollars, acting as a de facto buffer deployed by the Reserve Bank of India, but the intervention provided only partial relief.

The move brings the rupee back toward the multi-week pressure zone that prompted historic RBI intervention earlier in April. The currency has already lost nearly 1% in recent sessions and slipped well past the 92.50 level reached on the back of central bank support measures — the relief rally, as traders are now calling it, looks done.

---

### 02 — The Trigger: Hormuz Stays Functionally Shut

The Strait of Hormuz, through which approximately 20% of global energy supply transits, remains effectively closed. Iran has imposed restrictions on maritime energy flows as retaliation against U.S. naval pressure, while American military officials are simultaneously developing plans targeting Iranian capabilities in the strait. Iran has not agreed to resume peace talks with the U.S., blaming Washington for the continuous blockade of Iranian sea ports.

Brent settled above $100 for the first time in more than two weeks last Wednesday at $103.24, on stalled peace talks and continued restrictions on trade through the Strait of Hormuz fuelling supply concerns. ANZ Bank described the standoff as "a case of brinkmanship."

More than 20 million barrels of oil normally move through Hormuz each day and alternative pipeline capacity is limited, meaning a disruption affects both physical availability and pricing simultaneously. For markets, the closure has shifted from tail risk to live macro constraint.

---

### 03 — Asia FX: The Fault Line Between Buffers and Vulnerability

Tuesday's session saw broad weakness across oil-sensitive Asian currencies. Japan and South Korea are backed by deep strategic reserves and fiscal space to stabilise domestic fuel prices. By contrast, India, the Philippines, and Indonesia sit closer to the economic fault line, with thinner buffers and greater sensitivity to imported energy costs, according to analysts at ING.

After struggling at the bottom of the Asia FX pile till late March, the rupee gained about 2% since the first set of RBI measures were announced on March 27, becoming the second-best performer among major Asian peers in that window. In 2026 so far, though, the rupee remains bottom of the regional pile as investors continue to weigh weak capital flows and risks from elevated energy prices.

The broader Asia FX complex is simultaneously weighed upon by a firmer U.S. Dollar Index. The DXY trades near a 10-day high of around 99.00, as reviving inflationary fears temper expectations for a more dovish Federal Reserve and underpin the greenback.

---

### 04 — State-Run Banks as RBI's Proxy: The Dollar Sales Cushion

The defining feature of Tuesday's session was the presence of state-run banks selling dollars in spot markets — a well-understood mechanism through which the RBI manages volatility without directly and visibly burning through forex reserves. State-bank intervention is the central bank's preferred first-line tool, creating supply-side pressure against rupee depreciation.

This follows an escalating sequence of measures since late March. The RBI urged state-run oil refiners to curb spot dollar purchases and to tap a special credit line for their foreign exchange needs — a structural squeeze on the single largest source of dollar demand in India. The RBI also imposed a cap of $100 million per day on onshore open foreign exchange positions held by banks, effective April 10, explicitly designed to restrain speculative positioning against the rupee.

Just hours after the rupee weakened past 94 earlier this month, the RBI announced curbs on currency position limits to quell speculators. The rupee then jumped the most in 12 years as the RBI moved with some of its boldest steps in more than a decade. DBS Bank noted that "the risk of further incremental measures will keep aggressive rupee bears at bay."

---

### 05 — India's Macro Vulnerability: The Energy Tax

Union Bank of India's report titled *From Hormuz to the Rupee: War, Oil and the Global Repricing of Risk* states that higher oil keeps inflation risk alive, delays central bank easing, pressures current accounts, tightens financial conditions, and weighs on risk assets — especially in energy-importing economies.

The report warned that if disruptions persist, Brent is likely to hold in the $100–110 range, risking fuel price pass-through and CPI drifting above 4%. It also highlighted that every $10/barrel move in oil can significantly widen the current account deficit and push inflation higher.

The RBI's MPC maintained the policy repo rate at 5.25% while reiterating a neutral stance. India's merchandise trade deficit narrowed to $20.7 billion in March 2026, supported by lower bullion and energy imports — providing some external sector resilience.

India holds approximately 100 million barrels in strategic and commercial reserves — covering around 25 days of consumption, potentially stretchable to 40–45 days — but this buffer is finite and does not insulate the economy from price effects.

FIIs have remained net sellers for four consecutive trading days, offloading stakes worth ₹8,311.99 crore. Elevated oil prices have dimmed the interest of overseas investors in the Indian stock market amid concerns over India Inc.'s forward earnings and expectations that the government would trim capital expenditure to offset obligations towards rising energy prices.

---

### 06 — Technical Picture and Market Levels

The USD/INR pair trades firmly near the weekly high of 94.38, with the RSI at 59 — positive but not yet overbought — suggesting the advance could extend. On the downside, initial support is seen at the 20-period EMA at 93.35. A daily close below this floor would weaken the immediate bullish tone, while holding above it keeps the door open for further gains toward the all-time high of around 95.20.

MUFG's base case for the remainder of the quarter is a 94.00–95.00 range for USD/INR, with a risk scenario of 97.00–98.00 if oil prices remain elevated. UBS flagged that if elevated oil prices persist for even a couple of months, USD/INR could move toward 95 by Q2, assuming active RBI intervention continues.

In the near term, central bank measures have pulled the brake on one-way depreciation bias, with the rupee expected to trade between 92.50–94 per one FX salesperson at a foreign bank. At 94.47, the rupee has already broken above that ceiling.

---

### 07 — What to Watch

**Brent Crude** — The $100/bbl level is macro-critical. A sustained move above $105 would likely accelerate rupee pressure materially and raise the probability of fuel price pass-through domestically.

**Iran–U.S. Talks** — Any signal of resumed peace negotiations or a partial Hormuz reopening would be the single most powerful rupee-positive catalyst available. None is visible on the horizon.

**RBI Intervention Escalation** — Watch for further state-bank dollar sales, new refiners' credit line extensions, or tightened position limits as the next toolkit deployment. The RBI has demonstrated willingness to act aggressively, but each measure has diminishing marginal impact.

**FII Flows** — A return of net buying in Indian equities would ease currency pressure meaningfully. The current four-day selling streak is a significant secondary headwind.

**CPI Data** — Any upside surprise in headline CPI would constrain the RBI's ability to cut rates, removing a potential domestic growth tailwind at precisely the wrong moment.

**USD/INR 95** — The all-time high. A test of this level would likely trigger the most aggressive RBI response yet and re-price risk premium across Indian assets broadly.

---

### Bottom Line

The rupee's slide to 94.47 is not a disorderly rout — the RBI's accumulated credibility from April and ongoing state-bank intervention are preventing that. But it is a resumption of the structural weakening trend that the April relief rally only temporarily interrupted. The rupee is expected to trade with a mild depreciation bias, as elevated Brent crude prices, evolving geopolitical risks, and a firm U.S. dollar offset relatively stable domestic fundamentals.

The RBI is buying time, not buying structural relief. The central question for Indian macro over the next four to six weeks is whether the U.S.–Iran standoff de-escalates before the energy tax becomes severe enough to pass through into retail fuel prices, push CPI above 4%, and force a harder policy choice between defending the rupee and supporting growth. Until that question is answered, 94–95 remains the operative range to watch.

## Glossary

**Brent Crude:** The international benchmark price for oil, used to set energy prices globally. Most of India's oil imports are priced against Brent.

**CPI (Consumer Price Index):** India's main inflation measure. Food makes up ~50% of the basket, making it highly sensitive to supply shocks.

**Current Account Deficit:** The gap between what India earns from exports and what it spends on imports. Higher oil prices widen this gap because India imports ~85% of its oil.

**DXY (U.S. Dollar Index):** Measures the dollar's strength against a basket of major currencies. When DXY rises, most Asian currencies including the rupee tend to weaken.

**EMA (Exponential Moving Average):** A technical analysis tool that tracks average price over a set period, giving more weight to recent prices. Used to identify support and resistance levels.

**FII (Foreign Institutional Investor):** Overseas funds — mutual funds, pension funds, hedge funds — that invest in Indian stocks and bonds. When they sell, they convert rupees back to dollars, weakening the currency.

**Forex Reserves:** Foreign currency held by the RBI. Used to defend the rupee by selling dollars when the rupee weakens sharply.

**MPC (Monetary Policy Committee):** The RBI's six-member rate-setting committee that meets every two months to decide the repo rate.

**Repo Rate:** The interest rate at which the RBI lends to commercial banks — its primary policy lever for controlling inflation and growth.

**RSI (Relative Strength Index):** A momentum indicator used in technical analysis. Above 70 = overbought (likely to fall); below 30 = oversold (likely to rise). Currently at 59 for USD/INR — room to rise further.

**Strait of Hormuz:** A narrow waterway between Iran and Oman through which ~20% of global oil supply passes daily. Any closure immediately affects global energy prices.

**State Development Loans (SDL):** Bonds issued by Indian state governments to fund their spending. Heavy SDL supply competes with central government bonds and keeps yields elevated.

**USD/INR:** The exchange rate between the U.S. dollar and the Indian rupee. A higher number means the rupee has weakened — you need more rupees to buy one dollar.
