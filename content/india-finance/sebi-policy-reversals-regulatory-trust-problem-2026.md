## First — What Is SEBI and Why Does It Matter?

Think of SEBI — the Securities and Exchange Board of India — as the referee of India's financial markets. It sets the rules for how stocks, mutual funds, and derivatives are bought and sold. It protects ordinary investors from fraud. It decides what brokers can and cannot do. It is to India's stock market what a traffic department is to roads: without it, things would be chaotic.

SEBI was established in 1988 and given statutory powers in 1992. Its stated purpose is to protect the interests of investors in securities and to promote the development of, and regulate, the securities market.

For decades, it largely did that job well — building one of Asia's most sophisticated capital markets. But recently, something has been slipping: the consistency and predictability of its own rules.

---

## What Has Actually Been Happening

Over the past year, SEBI has introduced a series of significant regulatory changes — and then, after loud objections from brokers, traders, and industry groups, quietly walked some of them back.

Mint's reporting identifies at least **four policy reversals** following stakeholder pressure. Here are the most visible ones.

### The Bank Nifty Weekly Contracts Story

This is the clearest example of a rule that landed badly and caused immediate, measurable damage.

Bank Nifty is one of India's most widely traded financial instruments — an index that tracks the country's biggest banking stocks. For years, traders could buy and sell weekly options contracts on Bank Nifty, meaning bets that expired every week rather than every month. Millions of retail traders built entire strategies around these weekly contracts.

In late 2024, SEBI decided this was fuelling excessive speculation. The new index derivatives rules allowed each exchange to offer weekly derivatives contracts for only one benchmark index, beginning November 20, 2024. Under the new rules, NSE would offer weekly expiry only for the Nifty 50 index — discontinuing it for Nifty Bank, Nifty Financial Services, and Nifty Midcap Select indices.

The fallout was immediate. The broking community formally wrote to SEBI requesting the restoration of Bank Nifty weekly contracts. The National President of ANMI, the apex brokers' association, highlighted a **45% dip in options volume** since the change, which directly affected brokers' revenues.

*"We must also keep in mind that broking is also an industry. We are directly and indirectly giving employment to many people — the employment is at stake because of revenue reductions,"* the association's president said. The argument was not that the rule was wrong in principle — it was that SEBI had not adequately consulted the industry before pulling a product that millions of people depended on for their livelihood.

### The Expiry Day Shuffle

Then came the change to when derivatives contracts expire — a change that sounds technical but practically upended how every trader in India structured their week.

On September 1, 2025, SEBI mandated that all equity derivative contracts expire on either **Tuesdays or Thursdays** — ending a 25-year tradition where Thursday was synonymous with expiry day in India's markets. NSE shifted its entire F&O segment to Tuesday, while BSE adopted Thursday. The reasoning was legitimate — SEBI wanted to reduce the speculative frenzy that built up on shared expiry days. But traders who had built multi-year strategies around Thursday rhythms were given limited runway to adapt.

A proposal to move Bank Nifty to Monday was introduced but deferred in March 2025 — an early sign of SEBI testing ideas, facing resistance, and stepping back. That pattern would repeat.

### The Broader F&O Tightening

Underneath these specific changes sits a larger regulatory push: SEBI's crackdown on retail participation in the futures and options market. The data driving this was genuinely alarming. **SEBI's own research found that over 90% of retail F&O traders lost money over three years**, amounting to **₹1.81 trillion in aggregate losses**.

The response was a wave of new rules:
- Contract sizes were raised to ₹15–20 lakh
- Weekly expiry was restricted
- Option buyers were required to pay the full premium upfront from February 2025
- Intraday position monitoring with four random daily snapshots was introduced from April 2025

Each of these changes had a defensible rationale. The problem was the speed, sequencing, and lack of advance consultation — changes landing on top of changes before the market had absorbed the previous ones.

## The New Chairman's Unenviable Position

The timing of all this matters. **Tuhin Kanta Pandey** took charge as SEBI's 11th Chairman on March 1, 2025, replacing Madhabi Puri Buch, whose term ended on February 28, 2025. Many of the most aggressive F&O rules were introduced in the final months of the previous chairperson's tenure — leaving Pandey to manage the industry fallout from decisions he did not make.

His challenge: if he reverses the rules, he signals that SEBI's decisions are negotiable under pressure. If he holds firm, he risks ongoing damage to market volumes and broker viability. Neither option is comfortable for a regulator that depends on credibility to function.

## Why "Reversals" Are More Damaging Than They Sound

Here is the part that non-investors often miss: for financial markets, **regulatory predictability is almost as important as the rules themselves**.

Imagine you are a fund manager running ₹500 crore of other people's money. You build a strategy around certain market structures — weekly Bank Nifty contracts, specific margin rules, Thursday expiry rhythms. You invest in technology, hire people trained on those structures, and communicate your approach to clients.

Then SEBI changes three things simultaneously, partially walks one back after a month, adjusts another after industry lobbying, and hints at more changes ahead. The foundation you built on is shifting. Do you wait to see if this rule sticks before adapting? Do you build infrastructure for the new world, only for SEBI to reverse it?

This is not a hypothetical discomfort. It has a direct cost — in wasted investment, disrupted operations, and reduced confidence in the market as a stable place to do business.

The concern critics are raising is not that SEBI should never change its mind. Good regulators do consult, listen, and refine. The concern is about the sequence: **announcing a rule, implementing it, watching the damage, and then walking it back** suggests that proper consultation did not happen at the design stage — when it should.

---

## What Proper Consultation Actually Looks Like

Most mature regulators — in the UK, the US, Europe — follow a structured process:

1. Publish a **consultation paper** setting out the proposed rule and the reasoning behind it
2. Invite public comment for several weeks or months
3. Review the feedback
4. Publish a response explaining what they kept, changed, or rejected and why
5. Implement the rule with a reasonable transition period

SEBI does have this process. It has used it well in some areas — recent mutual fund regulations involved months of industry dialogue. But the F&O changes that caused the most disruption moved faster than the consultation process warranted — and the reversals that followed are evidence of that.

Consultation is not just courtesy. It is the mechanism by which regulators **learn what they do not know before a rule goes live**, rather than after.

---

## What This Means for Everyday Investors

If you invest in mutual funds, hold stocks, or have any exposure to India's capital markets — directly or through your provident fund — SEBI's decisions affect you, even if you never trade a derivative in your life.

A regulator that is seen as unpredictable pushes **foreign institutional investors** toward more stable markets, reducing capital flows into India. It raises compliance costs for brokers and fund houses, which can eventually pass through to the fees ordinary investors pay. And it creates a general sense of caution that slows down market innovation and product development.

On the other hand, SEBI's core concern — that over 90% of retail F&O traders are losing money — is legitimate and urgent. The question is not whether to act. It is whether the process of acting is good enough to avoid the collateral damage that messy reversals create.

The debate over SEBI's U-turns is ultimately a debate about **institutional quality**. A regulator that consults well, decides carefully, and rarely reverses course is a stronger institution than one that moves fast and corrects frequently — even if the final destination is the same.

## Glossary

**SEBI (Securities and Exchange Board of India):** India's capital markets regulator. It sets the rules for how stocks, mutual funds, and derivatives work, protects investors from fraud, and oversees brokers and fund houses. Think of it as the referee of India's financial system.

**F&O (Futures and Options):** A category of financial instruments called derivatives. Rather than buying a stock, you buy a contract that gives you the right or obligation to buy or sell a stock or index at a specific price by a specific date. Widely used for hedging and speculation.

**Derivatives:** Financial contracts whose value is derived from an underlying asset — a stock, index, commodity, or currency. They are tools for managing risk, but can also be used for high-risk speculation.

**Weekly Expiry / Bank Nifty Weekly Contracts:** Options contracts that expire every week rather than every month. Bank Nifty weekly options were enormously popular among retail traders for short-term strategies before SEBI removed them.

**Nifty 50 / Bank Nifty:** Major Indian stock market indices. Nifty 50 tracks India's 50 largest companies. Bank Nifty tracks the country's leading banking stocks. Both are heavily traded in the derivatives market.

**Consultation Paper:** A document published by a regulator that proposes a new rule, explains the reasoning, and invites public feedback before the rule is finalised. Considered best practice in mature regulatory systems.

**Margin:** The deposit a trader must put up as collateral when taking a position in derivatives. Higher margin requirements mean traders need more money to make the same bet.

**Regulatory Certainty:** The confidence that rules will remain stable and predictable over time. Crucial for businesses and investors to plan, invest, and operate without fear of sudden rule changes.

**Retail Traders:** Ordinary individuals trading in markets with their own money, as opposed to institutional traders (banks, funds) trading on behalf of clients or their own large balance sheets.

**Open Interest:** The total number of outstanding derivative contracts that have not been settled. High open interest means many traders hold active positions — a sign of market activity and liquidity.
