## The One-Line Answer

Inflation means your money buys less than it did before. ₹100 that bought you a bag of groceries last year might only buy 85% of that same bag today.

## Why Does Inflation Happen?

Think of the economy like a marketplace. When there are more buyers chasing the same number of goods, sellers raise prices. This happens for a few main reasons:

**1. Too much money in the system**
When the government prints more money or interest rates are very low, people have more cash to spend. More buyers + same supply = higher prices.

**2. Supply disruptions**
If a drought destroys wheat crops, less wheat is available. The same number of people want the same bread, but there's less wheat to make it — so prices rise.

**3. Higher input costs**
If oil prices shoot up (oil moves everything — trucks, factories, farms), the cost of making and shipping almost everything rises, and companies pass that cost to you.

## Who Measures and Controls It in India?

The **Reserve Bank of India (RBI)** — India's central bank — is officially responsible for keeping inflation between **2% and 6%**. They measure it using a number called **CPI (Consumer Price Index)**, which tracks the price of a fixed basket of everyday items: food, clothing, fuel, housing.

When inflation goes above 6%, the RBI raises **interest rates** (called the **Repo Rate**) to make borrowing expensive and cooling down spending. When inflation is too low, they cut rates to encourage spending.

## What This Means for Your Money

| Scenario | What Happens |
|---|---|
| Inflation at 6%, your savings account at 4% | You're actually losing 2% of buying power every year |
| Inflation falls, RBI cuts rates | Home loan EMIs get cheaper; good time to borrow |
| Inflation spikes | Stock markets often fall (uncertainty); gold usually rises |
| Inflation stable at 4% | Economy healthy; good for long-term investors |

## The India-Specific Angle

India's inflation is heavily influenced by **food prices** — food makes up about 46% of the CPI basket (much higher than the US or Europe). This means a bad monsoon or a vegetable price spike can push the headline inflation number up significantly, even if the rest of the economy is fine.

In 2024-25, India managed to bring retail inflation from 5.7% down to around 4.6%, helped by falling vegetable prices and stable fuel costs. The RBI cut its repo rate in February 2026 for the first time in nearly 5 years — from 6.5% to 6.25% — signalling confidence that inflation is under control.

## What Should You Do?

- **Don't keep too much in a savings account** earning 3-4% when inflation is at 5-6%. You're losing money in real terms.
- **Equity (stocks/mutual funds)** historically beats inflation over long periods — 10-12% CAGR vs 5-6% inflation.
- **Gold** is a classic inflation hedge — it tends to hold value when purchasing power falls.
- **Debt funds and FDs** make sense when real rates are positive (interest rate > inflation).

## The Global Picture

The US Federal Reserve (their version of RBI) fought a similar battle in 2022-23 when US inflation hit 9% — the highest in 40 years. They raised rates aggressively to 5.25-5.5%, eventually bringing inflation back to around 2.5% by late 2024. India's experience was milder but followed a similar arc.

## Jargon Glossary

**Inflation**: The rate at which prices rise over time, measured as a percentage. 5% inflation means prices are, on average, 5% higher than a year ago.

**CPI (Consumer Price Index)**: A number that tracks the average price of a fixed "basket" of common goods and services. When CPI goes up, prices are rising.

**RBI (Reserve Bank of India)**: India's central bank — controls money supply, sets interest rates, and manages the currency.

**Repo Rate**: The interest rate at which the RBI lends money to commercial banks. When it rises, all other interest rates in the economy tend to rise too (loans get expensive, saving gets rewarding).

**Real Rate of Return**: Your return minus inflation. A 7% FD when inflation is 6% gives you only 1% "real" return.

**CAGR (Compound Annual Growth Rate)**: The smoothed annual growth rate of an investment over multiple years. A ₹1 lakh investment growing to ₹2 lakhs in 7 years has a CAGR of ~10.4%.

**Monetary Policy**: The set of tools (mainly interest rates) that a central bank uses to control money supply and inflation.
