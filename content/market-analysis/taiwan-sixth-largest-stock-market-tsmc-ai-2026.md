*How one company — and one technology — rewrote the global financial rankings*

---

## The Big Picture: What Actually Happened?

Taiwan's stock market has overtaken Canada's to become the sixth-largest in the world. Taiwan's total market value surged over 35% this year to $4.47 trillion, while Canada's grew just 5% to $4.44 trillion. A gap that was hundreds of billions of dollars wide at the start of 2026 was erased in months.

The entire story comes down to one thing: artificial intelligence. AI requires enormous computing power, and the chips that power it are almost exclusively made in Taiwan — by a company called TSMC.

---

## The Climb: From 8th to 6th in Under Five Months

| Rank | When | What Happened |
|---|---|---|
| #8 | End of 2025 | Behind Germany, France, and Canada |
| #7 | January 2026 | Overtook France |
| **#6** | **April 2026** | **Overtook Canada. Index briefly hit 40,000 for the first time ever** |

For context: Taiwan's entire economy is worth about $977 billion — roughly a quarter of the UK's. Yet its stock market has now surpassed both France and Canada. That gap tells you how dramatically AI has inflated the value of chipmaking.

---

## The Driving Force: TSMC

TSMC — Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Company — is the world's largest contract chipmaker. It doesn't design chips; it manufactures them for companies like Apple, Nvidia, and AMD. Think of it as the world's most advanced factory, but for microscopic chips inside every smartphone, laptop, and AI server.

TSMC makes up nearly 45% of Taiwan's entire stock market by itself. When TSMC rises, the whole market rises with it.

**Q1 2026 Snapshot:**

| Metric | Result |
|---|---|
| Revenue | $35.9 billion — up 35% year-on-year |
| Profit growth | +58% — 4th straight quarter of record profits |
| Profit margin | 50.5% — keeps over half of every dollar it earns |
| Full-year 2026 guidance | Raised to above 30% growth |

AI chips now account for 61% of TSMC's revenue, up from around 40% just two years ago. Nvidia — the company behind chips that power most AI systems — is now TSMC's single largest customer. TSMC's CEO described AI demand as "extremely robust," and the company guided Q2 revenues of $39–40.2 billion, another 10% jump.

---

## The Policy Spark: A Rule Change That Unleashed Billions

Beyond earnings, a domestic policy decision poured fuel on the fire. Taiwan's financial regulator raised the limit on how much of a single stock an investment fund can hold — from 10% to 25%.

This matters because TSMC was previously "capped out" in many Taiwanese funds. The rule change immediately unlocked billions in new institutional buying. Analysts estimated it could channel over $6 billion into TSMC-linked investments over time. The day the announcement was made, Taiwan's index surged over 3% in a single session — with TSMC contributing 840 index points alone.

---

## The Bigger Picture: Taiwan's Economy Is Booming Too

The stock market rise isn't disconnected from reality. Taiwan's economy is genuinely firing on all cylinders.

- **GDP growth forecast:** 7.7% — matching India and Vietnam
- **Industrial production (March 2026):** +28.7% — fastest since May 2020
- **Per capita income** expected to hit $38,000 — surpassing Japan and South Korea for the first time

A bilateral trade deal with the US also cut tariffs on Taiwanese goods from 20% to 15%, easing one of the biggest investor concerns from 2025.

---

## Why Canada Fell Behind

Canada hasn't done anything wrong. Its market grew 5% this year — decent by any normal standard. The problem is structural: Canada's stock market is dominated by banks, oil companies, and commodity producers. None of these benefit from the AI boom the way semiconductor companies do.

This is really a story about two economic eras running simultaneously. Canada's market reflects the old economy — solid, stable, resource-driven. Taiwan's reflects the new one — where the most valuable thing in the world is the ability to manufacture billions of transistors on a sliver of silicon.

---

## What Could Go Wrong

**Too much riding on one company.** TSMC alone is 45% of Taiwan's entire market. If TSMC stumbles — on earnings, geopolitics, or competition — the whole market falls with it. The new fund rule allowing even more TSMC concentration makes this risk deeper, not shallower.

**The AI bubble question.** The IMF, the Bank of England, and investors like Ray Dalio have warned that AI-related valuations may be approaching unsustainable levels. If the AI boom slows or companies can't justify trillion-dollar infrastructure spending, Taiwan's market would be hit harder than almost any other.

**Geopolitical risk with China.** Cross-strait tensions remain a persistent background risk. A flare-up could cause capital to flee Taiwanese equities regardless of how strong the underlying earnings are.

**Future margin pressure.** TSMC is building fabs overseas, including in the US. This is expensive, and the company has flagged it could reduce profit margins by 2–4% over time.

---

## What Comes Next?

Goldman Sachs has forecast Taiwan's index will climb above 40,000 — a level briefly touched on April 28. With TSMC guiding $39–40 billion in Q2 revenue alone and full-year growth above 30%, the momentum remains strong.

Whether Taiwan cracks the top five globally will depend almost entirely on whether the AI infrastructure buildout continues at its current pace — and whether TSMC can build enough fabs to keep up with demand that already outstrips supply.

## Glossary

**Stock Market / Equity Market:** A marketplace where ownership stakes in companies are bought and sold. Its "size" refers to the combined value of all those companies.

**Market Capitalisation (Market Cap):** The total market value of a company or a country's entire stock market. Calculated by multiplying a share price by the total number of shares.

**TAIEX:** Taiwan's main stock market index — tracks the average performance of all major Taiwanese companies, like the Nifty 50 for India or the Dow Jones for the US.

**TSMC:** The world's largest contract chip manufacturer. It makes semiconductors designed by other companies but doesn't sell products under its own name. Almost every advanced chip in the world is made here.

**Semiconductor / Chip:** A tiny electronic component that processes information. Found in every modern device. The smaller the transistors, the more powerful the chip.

**Nanometre (nm):** One billionth of a metre. Chip transistors are now built at 3–7nm — roughly 10,000 times thinner than a human hair. Smaller means faster and more efficient.

**High-Performance Computing (HPC):** Computing tasks requiring enormous processing power — primarily AI training and data centre operations. The main reason AI companies need so many advanced chips.

**Revenue:** The total money a company earns before any costs are subtracted. Different from profit.

**Net Profit / Net Income:** What a company keeps after subtracting all costs from revenue. The "bottom line."

**Gross Margin:** The percentage of revenue left after direct production costs. TSMC's 66.2% means for every $100 of chips sold, $66.20 remains after manufacturing costs.

**Guidance / Forward Guidance:** A company's official prediction for its own future revenue or profits. When TSMC "raises guidance," it publicly increased its forecast — a strong sign of confidence.

**Institutional Investors:** Large professional organisations — pension funds, mutual funds, insurance companies — that invest on behalf of millions of individuals.

**Single-Stock Holding Cap:** A regulatory rule limiting how much of any one company's shares a fund can own. Taiwan raised this from 10% to 25%, unlocking billions in new TSMC buying.

**GDP (Gross Domestic Product):** The total value of all goods and services a country produces in a year. Measures the size of an economy — not the same as the size of its stock market.

**Concentration Risk:** The danger of having too much invested in one company or sector. If that one stock falls, everything falls with it.

**AI Bubble:** The concern that AI-related stocks are priced far beyond what their actual earnings justify — similar to the dot-com crash of 2000.

**Tariff:** A tax on imported goods imposed by one country on another. The US cut tariffs on Taiwanese goods from 20% to 15% in 2026, reducing costs for Taiwanese exporters.
